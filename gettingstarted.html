This page will serve to share my experience with blacksmithing, some of what I find interesting.


Some things about the trade

Basic Tools of The Trade

Hammer - usually around 1.5 - 2.5 pounds. A good all-around hammer would be a flat faced with
rounded oppoosite face. We then have a straight peen, cross peen, and ball (mechanic's hammer).
Personally, I used a 4lb. short sledge.

Tongs - There are a plethora and a half of tongs one could use. The prefered and sought after
are called Wolf Jaw tongs. These are able to hold most common bar stock up to an inch, on the X
and Z axis. For the Y axis, we can simply rotate our wrist 90 degrees.

File or Rasp - Allows for material removal in specific areas where a hammer can't. Despite the
answer to all of life's question is to hammer it out, it's not always the best answer.

Quench Tub - Water is great for most things, but for fine tools, you should seek another
tub for oil. Used motor oil is a cheap source.

Anvil - Perhaps the most expensive tool. Ranging upwards of $,5000 for the BEST. Alternatively,
in a pinch, a chunk of rail from the railroad.

FIRE!! - You can beat metals cold but only so much before they become too brittle or too thin.
Fun fact, non-ferrous metals like copper, HARDEN when hammered or worked. You need fire to heat, then
quench them (in water) to aneal (return to base softness) them to work furhter. Where as ferrous
metals require heating and radiative cooling (just leave it alone soomewhere) to aneal it.

Apron - What...don't want to get too dirty, do you?

Some important Things To Practice

Accuracy - Being able to hit the mark is important. If you can't hit the metal, you'll hit the
anvil and dent it. And if you're standing in the wrong spot over the work, it WILL bounce back and
smack you in the face.

Timing - You may have seen videos where a smith taps his hammer rhythmically between hammer swings.
This is to create momentum to bring the hammer back up after needing a moment to plan the next hit, or,
when working with others on a large pace, a signal as to when they should swing their hammer. This
technique is used when training an apprecntice to teach them ques.

Visual Temperatures - Low-light is a smith's best friend with temperatures. We learn about how
metal changes color based on heat. Anyone who knows anything about crystaline structures & chemistry
knows rxactly what I am referencing. The common item we forge, steel, goes from a dull read at about
500 degrees, to sparking and burning (yes you can burn metal) at 2300 degrees. As we approach the upper
temperature, we would be able to do forge welding, two peices are heated and then hammered together.

Things To Make

Trinkets - Charm bracelets and charms, keychains, abstract items or representation.

Fire Place Screens - Beautiful 3-D art to protect people from getting into a fire, and the fire
spilling out, too.

Gates - Think driveways and walkway or garden gates.

Fences - To go with the gates or just a beautiful fence on its own.

Everyday Items - Cooking utensil, candle holders, lighting, shelves, tables, etc...

Historical Items - Swords, knives, armor, and the like.
